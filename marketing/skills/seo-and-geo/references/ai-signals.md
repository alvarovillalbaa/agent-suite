# AI Signals: E-E-A-T, Trust, and Writing Quality Reference

How to build content AI systems trust — and avoid patterns that undermine credibility.

---

## E-E-A-T: Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness

Google's quality evaluation framework. Now the foundation for AI citation decisions too.

### Experience

Demonstrated first-hand experience with the topic.

**Signals:**
- Personal case studies ("We tested 12 CRM tools over 6 months and found...")
- Original screenshots and custom images (not stock photos)
- Specific numbers from real usage ("Our conversion rate went from 2.1% to 3.8%")
- Admitted failures and limitations ("This approach didn't work because...")
- Behind-the-scenes process documentation

**Missing experience signals:**
- Generic content that could describe any situation
- Stock photos where original photography would be expected
- Vague claims without specifics ("This strategy increased our results significantly")
- No concrete examples or applications

### Expertise

Depth of knowledge demonstrated in the content.

**Signals:**
- Technically accurate use of domain terminology
- Explanation of nuances and edge cases
- Awareness of common mistakes and how to avoid them
- References to primary sources (studies, official docs, regulations)
- Demonstrated knowledge of the topic's history and evolution

**Author expertise signals:**
- Author biography with relevant credentials, certifications, job titles
- Author page listing their body of work in this domain
- Citations of the author's work by other publications
- Social proof: speaking at conferences, published books, academic affiliations

### Authoritativeness

Recognition from others in your field.

**Signals:**
- Backlinks from high-authority domains in your industry
- Brand mentions in news, industry publications, podcasts
- Citations of your data/research by others
- Social following and engagement from recognized experts
- Wikipedia mentions (for established brands)
- Presence in "top X" lists by respected publishers

**Build authoritativeness:**
- Original research that others cite
- Guest posting on established industry publications
- Expert interviews that get shared
- Building tools that others link to and use
- Community involvement (forums, GitHub, professional organizations)

### Trustworthiness

The most important E-E-A-T signal per Google's guidelines.

**Signals:**
- HTTPS with valid SSL certificate
- Visible and accurate contact information
- Physical address (for local businesses)
- About page with organizational transparency
- Privacy policy and terms of service
- Correction policy (for editorial content)
- Clear disclosure of sponsored/affiliate content
- Author bios on content
- Sources and citations for factual claims
- No deceptive ads or misleading design

**Red flags that damage trust:**
- No contact information
- No About page
- No author attribution
- Claims without sources
- Deceptive advertising practices
- Inconsistent information across pages
- Old content without update dates

## AI-Specific Trust Signals

AI systems have their own trust hierarchies that partly differ from Google's.

### What AI Systems Prefer to Cite

**High citation probability:**
- Wikipedia and Wikimedia content
- Government and official institutional websites (.gov, .edu)
- Major news publications (NYT, Reuters, BBC, WSJ)
- Peer-reviewed journals and academic institutions
- Official documentation (docs.python.org, developer.mozilla.org)
- Recognized industry research firms (Gartner, Forrester, Statista)

**Moderate citation probability:**
- Established industry blogs and publications (HubSpot, Moz, SEMrush Blog)
- Well-known tool documentation
- Established brands with domain authority
- Content with specific statistics from named sources

**Low citation probability:**
- New or unknown domains
- Content without author attribution
- Content without dates
- Content without external citations
- Content that appears AI-generated

### The Named Source Effect

AI systems strongly prefer content with named, credible attributions.

**Without named source (lower citation probability):**
> Studies show that 70% of marketers struggle with content consistency.

**With named source (higher citation probability):**
> According to HubSpot's 2024 State of Marketing Report, 68% of marketers cite content consistency as their primary challenge, a 12% increase from 2023.

### The Specificity Signal

Specific, verifiable claims are trusted more than vague assertions.

**Vague (low AI citation value):**
> SEO can significantly improve your traffic over time.

**Specific (high AI citation value):**
> SEO produces compounding returns: Ahrefs' 2023 analysis of 920 million pages found that 90.63% of pages get zero organic traffic, while top-ranking pages capture 30-40x more traffic than position 5.

## AI Writing Detection: Patterns to Avoid

AI systems and human readers are increasingly sensitive to AI-generated writing patterns. Avoid these to maintain credibility.

### Em Dashes (Primary AI Tell)

The em dash (—) is the most reliable marker of AI-generated content. AI models drastically overuse them.

**Instead of em dashes, use:**
- Commas for parenthetical information
- Colons to introduce explanations
- Parentheses for supplementary context
- Separate sentences when ideas need space

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|------------|
| The results—which were surprising—showed... | The results, which were surprising, showed... |
| This approach—unlike traditional methods—allows... | This approach, unlike traditional methods, allows... |
| Communication skills—both written and verbal—are essential | Communication skills (both written and verbal) are essential |

**Rule:** If you write more than one em dash per page, revise.

### Overused AI Verbs

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|------------|
| delve (into) | explore, examine, investigate, look at |
| leverage | use, apply, draw on |
| optimize | improve, refine, enhance |
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help, enable, support |
| foster | encourage, support, develop |
| bolster | strengthen, support, reinforce |
| underscore | emphasize, highlight, stress |
| unveil | reveal, show, introduce |
| navigate | manage, handle, work through |
| streamline | simplify, make more efficient |
| endeavour | try, attempt |
| ascertain | find out, determine |
| elucidate | explain, clarify |

### Overused AI Adjectives

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|------------|
| robust | strong, reliable, thorough |
| comprehensive | complete, thorough, full |
| pivotal | key, critical, central |
| crucial | important, key, essential |
| transformative | significant, important, major |
| cutting-edge | new, advanced, recent |
| groundbreaking | new, original, significant |
| innovative | new, original, creative |
| seamless | smooth, easy, effortless |
| holistic | complete, whole |
| nuanced | subtle, complex, detailed |
| multifaceted | complex, varied |

### Overused AI Transitions

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|------------|
| furthermore | also, in addition, and |
| moreover | also, and, besides |
| that being said | however, but, still |
| at its core | essentially, basically |
| to put it simply | in short |
| it is worth noting that | note that, importantly |
| in the realm of | in, within |
| in today's [anything] | currently, now, today |
| notwithstanding | despite, even so |

### Structural Patterns That Signal AI

**Opening phrases to avoid:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In today's digital age..."
- "In an era of..."
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- "It's important to note that..."
- "Let's delve into..."
- "Imagine a world where..."

**Transitional phrases to avoid:**
- "That being said..."
- "With that in mind..."
- "It's worth mentioning that..."
- "This begs the question..."

**Concluding phrases to avoid:**
- "In conclusion..."
- "To sum up..."
- "By [doing X], you can [achieve Y]..."
- "At the end of the day..."

**Sentence patterns to avoid:**
- "Whether you're a [X], [Y], or [Z]..." (three-part "whether" list)
- "It's not just [X], it's also [Y]..."
- Starting sentences with "By" + gerund: "By understanding X, you can Y..."

### Filler Words That Weaken Claims

Remove or replace with specific alternatives:

- absolutely, actually, basically, certainly, clearly
- definitely, essentially, extremely, fundamentally
- incredibly, interestingly, naturally, obviously
- quite, really, significantly, simply, surely
- truly, ultimately, undoubtedly, very

**Instead of:** "This is really important..."
**Write:** "This is the highest-impact change you can make..."

## Human Writing Quality Signals

Content that reads as human-written earns more trust from both search engines and AI systems.

### Voice Consistency

Every article should have a consistent point of view and personality. Readers (and AI systems) can detect when content switches between styles.

**Signals of authentic voice:**
- Consistent use of first or third person throughout
- Specific opinions stated and defended
- Occasional informal language that matches the audience
- Willingness to disagree with conventional wisdom when justified
- Personal anecdotes or observations where appropriate

### Structural Variety

AI-generated content tends toward uniform paragraph length and sentence structure.

**Human variety signals:**
- Mix of short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones
- Varied paragraph length (some 1 sentence, some 5-6)
- Occasional questions posed to the reader
- Direct second-person address in published web content when instructional ("You'll need to..." works in a blog post, not in skill documentation)
- Section breaks that feel organic, not formulaic

### Concrete Over Abstract

AI writing tends toward abstraction. Human writing tends toward specificity.

**Abstract (AI pattern):**
> Implementing these strategies will lead to meaningful improvements in your organic performance metrics.

**Concrete (human pattern):**
> After implementing hreflang tags across our 12 language variants, our organic traffic from France grew 43% in three months — with no other changes.

## Self-Check for AI Writing Detection

Before publishing:

1. Read the content aloud. If any phrase sounds unnatural in speech, revise it
2. Ask: "Would I say this exact phrase in a meeting with a colleague?"
3. Count em dashes — if more than one per 1,000 words, revise
4. Search for words from the "avoid" lists above
5. Check that every paragraph contains at least one specific detail (number, name, example)
6. Verify all factual claims have named sources
7. Confirm the author's voice is consistent throughout
8. Ensure at least one original insight that wasn't in your sources

## Content Trust Audit Checklist

**Author signals:**
- [ ] Author name visible on page
- [ ] Author has a bio with real credentials
- [ ] Author bio links to social proof (Twitter/X, LinkedIn, published works)
- [ ] Author page on site links to all their articles

**Factual credibility:**
- [ ] Every key statistic cites a named source
- [ ] All claims are accurate and verifiable
- [ ] No outdated statistics (check dates)
- [ ] No exaggerated or unsupported superlatives

**Organizational trust:**
- [ ] HTTPS active on the page
- [ ] About page exists and is thorough
- [ ] Contact information accessible
- [ ] Privacy policy linked in footer
- [ ] Disclosure of commercial relationships where relevant

**Content freshness:**
- [ ] Publication date visible
- [ ] Last updated date visible if content has been revised
- [ ] No references to the current year as a future year (e.g., "In 2024..." when it's 2026)
- [ ] Screenshots and UI examples match current product/tool state

**Writing quality:**
- [ ] No AI writing pattern words from the lists above
- [ ] No em dash overuse
- [ ] Voice is consistent throughout
- [ ] At least one concrete specific per section
- [ ] Reads naturally aloud
