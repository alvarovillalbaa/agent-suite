# Follow-Up Triage

Use this reference when the user asks who needs a reply, which deals or threads need a bump, what promises they made that still need follow-through, or when triaging an inbox before deciding where to draft.

## Email classification framework

Before scanning for follow-up needs, classify emails into four buckets. This prevents wasting drafting effort on noise.

### Urgent
Time-sensitive — act first:
- "Urgent", "ASAP", "time-sensitive" in subject or body
- From a manager, executive, or client
- Deadline or access issue mentioned
- Security or billing alert requiring verification

### Needs response
Real people expecting a reply:
- Direct question from a human
- Request for input, a deliverable, or a decision
- Active thread the sender is participating in
- From a VIP: manager, client, partner, investor

### FYI (archive after review)
Useful but no reply needed:
- Receipts, confirmations, shipping notifications
- Calendar responses that require no action
- Automated reports or dashboards
- Security alerts where no action is required

### Auto-archive
No action, no read required:
- Marketing emails with unsubscribe links
- Newsletters not recently opened
- Automated platform notifications (GitHub, LinkedIn, analytics)
- Any sender the user has consistently ignored

**Safety rules when triaging automatically:**
- Never send an email without explicit user permission
- Never delete — archive only
- When uncertain whether to archive, surface it to the user rather than hiding it
- If the user un-archives something from a sender, remember that sender for future runs

## Scan order

Check these in order:

1. unanswered inbound emails or messages from real people
2. promises made in sent mail or CRM notes
3. stale threads where the sender may need to re-open the conversation
4. overdue tasks labeled as follow-up or waiting

## Unanswered inbound

Prioritize messages that are:

- older than 1-2 business days
- from a human, not an automated sender
- asking a direct question
- requesting a deliverable, intro, or decision
- tied to a clear deadline or time window

Exclude:

- newsletters, marketing blasts, noreply mail, and automated alerts
- low-signal "thanks" replies with no action implied
- replies that should stay with another owner unless the user asked to take them over

## Promises made

Search recent sent messages and notes for commitment phrases such as:

- "I'll send you..."
- "I'll follow up..."
- "Let me get back to you..."
- "Will do"
- "I'll have it by..."
- "Give me until..."

For each promise, capture:

- what was promised
- to whom
- when it was promised
- whether there is evidence the promise was fulfilled

If fulfillment is unclear, label it clearly rather than assuming the work was done.

## Stale-thread logic

A stale thread usually means:

- the user sent the last message
- the other side has not replied
- at least 3 business days have passed for warm/active opportunities
- the original reason to reach out still matters

Choose the bump angle based on what is missing:

- **New proof:** a fresh customer story, result, or resource
- **Sharper framing:** a clearer articulation of the pain or reason to care
- **Softer CTA:** an easier yes/no or permission-based ask
- **Close-the-loop:** respectful permission to disengage if timing is off

Do not send a bump that simply repeats the last message with different wording.

## Output expectations

When returning a follow-up queue, include:

- the item
- why it needs attention now
- the best next action
- whether a draft should be created immediately

If the user wants help acting on the queue, pick the 1-3 highest-leverage items and draft those first.

## Tone

- helpful, not guilt-inducing
- concrete about deadlines and ownership
- honest when evidence is incomplete
- action-oriented: reply now, bump, close the loop, or dismiss
