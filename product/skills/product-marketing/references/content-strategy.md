# Content Strategy Reference

Use this reference when the user needs to plan, prioritize, or structure content that drives traffic, builds authority, and generates leads.

Every content decision should be either **searchable** (capturing existing demand) or **shareable** (creating new demand). Build on search as the foundation, then layer shareability.

---

## Searchable vs. Shareable Content

| Type | Goal | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Searchable | Capture existing demand via keyword optimization | Use-case pages, comparison guides, template libraries |
| Shareable | Create demand via novel insights and thought leadership | Original research, expert roundups, case studies, meta content |

### Searchable content formats

- **Use-case content** — persona + use-case combinations targeting specific problem searches
- **Hub-and-spoke architecture** — one comprehensive overview page (hub) linked to related subtopic articles (spokes); builds topical authority and internal link equity
- **Template libraries** — high-intent keyword targets for users searching template-based solutions

### Shareable content formats

- **Thought leadership** — articulates a concept your audience hasn't named yet; earns backlinks and shares
- **Data-driven content** — original research from product data, public datasets, or proprietary surveys
- **Expert roundups** — 15–30 practitioners answer one focused question; distributes promotion across contributors
- **Case studies** — structured as Challenge → Solution → Results → Learnings; best for decision-stage buyers
- **Meta content** — behind-the-scenes transparency (process, culture, numbers); builds trust and humanizes the brand

## Content Pillars Framework

Identify 3–5 core pillars before creating individual pieces. Use all four lenses to validate each pillar:

1. **Product-led** — problems your product directly solves
2. **Audience-led** — knowledge gaps and questions your ICP has
3. **Search-led** — topic clusters with meaningful search volume
4. **Competitor-led** — topics where competitors rank that you should own

Each pillar spawns a subtopic cluster: a hub article plus interconnected spoke articles. Map the cluster before writing to avoid content sprawl.

## Keyword Research by Buyer Stage

Match keyword intent to the buyer's current decision stage:

| Stage | Intent signals | Example formats |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Learning, exploring | "what is X", "how to X", "guide to X" |
| Consideration | Comparing options | "best X", "X vs Y", "X alternatives" |
| Decision | Ready to buy | "X pricing", "X reviews", "X demo" |
| Implementation | Just purchased | "X templates", "X tutorial", "X setup guide" |

Map each content idea to a stage before writing. Awareness content builds volume; decision content closes.

## Content Ideation Sources

Prioritize sources with the highest signal-to-noise ratio:

1. **Keyword data** — Ahrefs, SEMrush, Google Search Console exports; reveals actual search demand
2. **Sales and customer call transcripts** — the most honest signal of objections, pain language, and decision drivers
3. **Survey responses** — quantified pain points; useful for data-driven posts
4. **Forum research** — Reddit, Quora, Indie Hackers; reveals unfiltered audience language and recurring frustrations
5. **Competitor analysis** — audit competitor content for gaps, ranking keywords, and neglected angles
6. **Sales and support team input** — frontline teams hear objections and questions daily; debrief regularly

## Prioritization Matrix

Score each content idea on four weighted factors. Use a 1–10 scale per factor.

| Factor | Weight | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| Customer Impact | 40% | Does this directly address a pain point or buying decision? |
| Content-Market Fit | 30% | Does the format and angle match how this audience consumes content? |
| Search Potential | 20% | Is there measurable search demand and a realistic ranking opportunity? |
| Resource Requirements | 10% | How much effort does it take to produce and promote? (Invert: lower effort = higher score) |

**Priority Score** = (Customer Impact × 0.4) + (Content-Market Fit × 0.3) + (Search Potential × 0.2) + (Resource Requirements × 0.1)

Sequence content by Priority Score within each pillar. Deprioritize high-effort, low-impact work even if it feels strategically interesting.

## Survey-Driven Lead Magnets

When survey responses are available, treat them as a segmentation input, not just a quote source.

Use survey data to:
- cluster recurring pain points
- identify which pain is both common and commercially relevant
- choose the best lead magnet format for that segment
- create an acquisition offer that mirrors real audience language

Best-fit lead magnet formats:

| Pain type | Best format |
|---|---|
| Multi-step operational problem | Checklist |
| Strategic or nuanced problem | Guide |
| Repeated task or workflow gap | Template |
| ROI, pricing, or efficiency tradeoff | Calculator |
| Creative or copy problem | Swipe file |

If the request is primarily about turning raw survey data into ranked lead magnet opportunities, load `survey-to-lead-magnet.md` directly instead of staying at the general content-strategy layer.

## Content Strategy Output Contract

When producing a content strategy, always deliver:

### 1. Content pillars

For each pillar:
- Pillar name and one-line rationale
- Lens(es) that validate it (product-led, audience-led, search-led, competitor-led)
- Hub article title and target keyword

### 2. Priority topics

For each recommended topic:
- Title and target keyword
- Buyer stage
- Content type (searchable or shareable, specific format)
- Priority Score with factor breakdown

### 3. Topic cluster map

- Hub article at center
- Spoke articles with brief description and internal link relationship
- Gaps in current cluster (existing content that needs updating or cross-linking)

## Failure modes to avoid

- Producing a topic list without mapping it to buyer stage or funnel position
- Treating all content as equally searchable — shareable content must be genuinely novel
- Building pillar clusters without auditing existing content first
- Skipping keyword validation on "obviously good" topics
- Hub articles that are too broad to rank for anything specific
- Expert roundups with generic questions that produce generic answers
- Case studies without quantified results ("we improved performance" vs. "we reduced load time by 43%")
- Prioritizing search volume over customer impact — high-volume, low-intent content drains resources
- Content calendars without a promotion and distribution plan per piece
