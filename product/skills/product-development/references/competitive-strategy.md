# Competitive Strategy

Load this file when the request is about competitive landscape analysis, product positioning, market maps, response options, or strategy implications from competitor movement.

## Start with the real comparison set

Do not compare against every company in the category.

Identify:

- direct competitors for the same buyer and same job
- indirect alternatives or workarounds
- internal substitute behaviors such as spreadsheets, email, agencies, or manual ops

The relevant set is the one that appears in an actual buying or adoption decision.

## Competitive brief workflow

### 1. Define the segment and job

- who is buying or adopting
- what job they are trying to get done
- what trigger makes them evaluate options now

### 2. Choose comparison dimensions

Use dimensions that change the decision, such as:

- speed to value
- depth versus simplicity
- workflow fit
- integration fit
- reliability or trust
- analytics or control
- service or implementation burden
- price model or total cost

Avoid generic scorecards with arbitrary categories.

### 3. Compare honestly

For each competitor or alternative, state:

- where they are stronger
- where they are weaker
- where they are optimized for a different segment

Do not force "we win everywhere." If that were true, the market would already show it.

### 4. Turn analysis into decisions

End with implications for:

- positioning and narrative
- roadmap priorities
- launch timing
- pricing or packaging
- sales objections and proof points

If the analysis does not change a decision, it is too abstract.

## Output template

Use this structure for competitive analysis:

1. Segment and buying trigger
2. Relevant alternatives
3. Comparison dimensions
4. Strengths, weaknesses, and tradeoffs
5. Differentiation statement
6. Strategic implications
7. Evidence gaps and what would change the conclusion

## Positioning guidance

Strong positioning:

- starts from a narrow segment with a live problem
- makes one sharp claim the market can remember
- is supported by evidence, proof, or product reality

Weak positioning:

- tries to be better for everyone
- lists features instead of a reason to choose
- ignores where competitors are actually advantaged

## Decision rules

- If a competitor is stronger on core buyer criteria, do not mirror them blindly. Pick a narrower segment, a different wedge, or a different proof model.
- If competitors are converging, differentiation may need to come from workflow, service model, or speed to value rather than raw feature count.
- If the strongest alternative is "do nothing" or "keep the workaround," focus on change cost and urgency, not category chest-thumping.
