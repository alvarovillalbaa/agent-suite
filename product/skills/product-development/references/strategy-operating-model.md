# Strategy Operating Model

Load this file when the request is about quarterly planning, annual direction, OKRs, product vision, roadmap themes, or product team scaling.

## Pick one strategy type first

Choose the primary lens before drafting goals:

- `growth`: acquire more users, expand markets, improve activation, or increase reach
- `retention`: reduce churn, improve habit strength, increase repeat usage, or improve customer health
- `revenue`: increase ARPU, monetization efficiency, conversion to paid, or expansion revenue
- `innovation`: create differentiation, open a new wedge, or invest in capability-led advantage
- `operational`: improve speed, reliability, focus, execution quality, or organizational leverage

Do not present all five as equal priorities. Pick one primary lens, note one secondary lens at most, and state what is deprioritized.

## Quarterly strategic planning workflow

### 1. Define the planning context

- What changed now
- What company goal or market shift this planning cycle must support
- What the product team can realistically influence this cycle

### 2. Gather baseline and target metrics

Prefer concrete current and target values for the primary outcome and 1-3 support metrics.

Examples:

- MAU: current -> target
- retention: current -> target
- activation rate: current -> target
- NPS or CSAT: current -> target
- paid conversion: current -> target
- release cycle time: current -> target

If the user lacks baselines, state that plainly and avoid fake precision.

### 3. Draft the cascade

Reason top-down:

1. Company outcomes
2. Product outcomes
3. Team outcomes

At each level:

- keep 3-5 objectives max
- keep 3-5 key results per objective max
- use measurable key results
- make the parent linkage explicit

### 4. Review alignment

Check four dimensions:

- `vertical alignment`: product and team objectives clearly support a parent objective
- `horizontal alignment`: teams do not work at cross-purposes and dependencies are named
- `coverage`: major company goals that product should influence are represented
- `balance`: one team is not carrying most of the plan without justification

Heuristics:

- `>80%` overall alignment is healthy
- `60-80%` needs refinement
- `<60%` likely needs restructuring

These numbers are heuristics, not math theater. Use them to force explicit discussion of gaps.

### 5. Refine and operationalize

Before finalizing:

- remove duplicate or conflicting objectives
- adjust team assignments for realistic capacity
- call out cross-functional dependencies
- define weekly or bi-weekly review cadence
- decide what evidence would trigger a strategy change mid-cycle

## OKR writing rules

- Objectives should describe the outcome to achieve, not the project to complete
- Key results should be measurable and time-bound
- Prefer `increase X from A to B` over vague improvement language
- Use contribution logic when product is only part of the company result
- If a team mainly enables others, state that role instead of forcing fake ownership

## Strategy brief template

Use this structure when the user wants a quarterly or annual strategy artifact:

1. Strategic context
2. Primary strategy type and why now
3. Outcome statement
4. Strategic bets
5. Roadmap themes
6. Metrics and review cadence
7. Risks, tradeoffs, and explicit deprioritization

## Vision and roadmap guidance

For vision documents:

- describe the user future state, not just the feature set
- explain why the company can credibly win
- connect the vision to 12-18 month roadmap themes
- keep principles stable even if tactics change

For roadmap themes:

- group work by outcome or problem space, not by project dump
- show sequencing logic and dependencies
- say what is not being pursued this period

## Team-scaling guidance

Use outcome-based org design, not status-based org design.

Start with:

- current team size and manager bandwidth
- major product surfaces or missions
- where coordination friction already exists
- which capabilities are missing versus merely stretched

Good patterns:

- organize around persistent missions or user journeys
- separate platform or enablement work only when it truly serves multiple teams
- add specialists only when their leverage is repeatable and material

Warnings:

- more teams increase coordination cost
- leadership layers should solve span-of-control problems, not create prestige titles
- do not hire ahead of a strategy that is still unclear

A scaling proposal should answer:

- what outcome improves
- why the current structure cannot support it well enough
- what the new structure changes in decision speed or ownership clarity
- what risk the new structure introduces
