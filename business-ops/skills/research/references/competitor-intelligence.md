# Competitor Intelligence

Use this lane when the user needs to understand how a competitor competes, how
the market is shifting, or how to position against alternative choices.

## Objective

Answer one decision-oriented question:

- where are we exposed
- where can we win
- what changed recently
- how should product, marketing, or sales respond

## Required Scope

Define:

- our company or reference product
- the competitor set
- whether the task is strategic, GTM, product, or sales enablement
- the comparison dimensions that matter
- the freshness requirement

Classify competitors as:

- `direct`
- `indirect`
- `adjacent`
- `emerging`

If the user names only one competitor, still note the category and whether the
real decision involves other alternatives.

## Source Stack

Start with this order:

1. homepage, product pages, solution pages
2. pricing and packaging
3. docs, changelog, release notes, templates, or product tour
4. customer stories and proof points
5. jobs, hiring plans, and organizational signals
6. newsroom, funding, acquisitions, partnerships
7. review sites and community complaints
8. analyst or press framing when it materially affects buying behavior

Always date-stamp evidence. Competitive intelligence decays quickly.

## What To Extract

For each competitor, capture:

- who they say they serve
- what category they claim
- their core promise
- differentiators they claim
- proof points they use
- pricing motion and packaging clues
- product strengths
- obvious gaps or weaknesses
- recent moves that suggest strategic direction

Also capture:

- what buyers likely like about them
- what buyers likely complain about
- where their message is strong
- where their message is vague, generic, or fragile

## Mandatory Outputs

### 1. Quick read

Provide:

- one-sentence description of the competitor
- why they matter now
- threat level: `high`, `medium`, or `low`

### 2. Messaging matrix

Use these rows:

- tagline or headline
- target customer
- category framing
- value proposition
- differentiator claim
- proof points
- CTA

### 3. Product and GTM comparison

Use these rows:

- product scope
- ease of adoption
- enterprise readiness
- pricing motion
- self-serve versus sales-led
- ecosystem or integrations
- trust and compliance signals

### 4. Battlecard section

Always include:

- where they are genuinely strong
- where they are weak
- where we can differentiate credibly
- landmines to set
- landmines to defuse

### 5. Watchlist

List the few changes worth monitoring:

- hiring patterns
- pricing changes
- category reframing
- release velocity
- partnerships
- customer proof or social proof spikes

## Judgment Rules

- be honest about where the competitor is better
- do not confuse marketing copy with demonstrated capability
- use review sites for sentiment themes, not exact truth
- do not recommend attacking a competitor on a dimension where we are weak
- prefer differentiators tied to customer outcomes, not feature checklist trivia

## Recommended Close

End with:

1. what this means for positioning
2. what this means for product or roadmap
3. what this means for sales talk tracks
4. what should be monitored next
